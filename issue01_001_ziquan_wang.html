The Life of Fish in Chi Wah Learning Commons

Wang Ziquan
Bsc( Biochemistry), Faculty of Science, University of Hong Kong, HK SAR

The marine aquarium located on the 2nd floor of Chi Wah Learning Commons (CWLC) is a beautiful spot. Assertedly, it is donated by Prof. John Malpas, the HKU Pro-Vice-Chancellor (2000 - 2013), who has spent millions of dollars on its maintenance. If that is the case, it becomes important to investigate the real upkeep conditions. What are the fishes and their living conditions? How good is the water quality? These topics are important to be discussed considering the mental and physical health of the fish.

Fish Species in CWLC

There are eight fish members currently living in the tank, which are five clownfish (Amphiprion ocellaris), one blue tang (Paracanthurus hepatus), one yellow tang (Zebrasoma flavescens), and one Indian sailfin tang (Zebrasoma desjardinii). The yellow tang is the most expensive fish in CWLC, which is around 5000 HKD. Next time you visit CWLC, you may recognize them according to Fig1.

The terms “clownfish” and “tang” are trade names representing their families (Pomacentridae and Acanthuridae). You can see members from these two families in almost every aquarium since they are highly adaptable and ornamental. In this article, we will use their commercial names for convenience.

Mental Health of the Clownfish and Tangs

Today, most people know that clownfish live in anemones thanks to the movie Finding Nemo. This symbiotic relationship is necessary in nature since anemones protect clownfish from their enemy and are the key to their family hierarchy [1]. Naturally, clownfish need to compete for anemone shelter, and the winner will develop into a female with a larger body size and family status. However, there is no anemone in the CWLC aquarium, so the clownfish are living without competition.

Tangs are more susceptible to disease than clownfish because they are more sensitive to stress. The word “stress” is confusing. Some may associate it with pressure, while others may infer it as ion stress. Actually, the answer is both. Fish will get stressed just like humans if they are in a narrow space or have bad roommates. Skilled aquarists always adjust the species of fish carefully to avoid potential violence. For example, odd-looking boxfish are discriminated against by tangs so they cannot live together.

As for the aquarium in CWLC, the space is enough for fish to swim. Also, clownfish and tangs are highly compatible species, which means they can live together blithely. Therefore, the mental health of fish is shown to be fine in CWLC. However, besides the spiritual living standards, water quality is also significant. The seawater environment involves complex chemistry, which determines the physical health of fish.

Water Parameters and Fish’s Physical Health

The major difference between seawater and freshwater is, apparently, the salt content. In chemistry, salt refers to ionic compounds consisting of a positively charged ion (cation) of a base and a negatively charged ion (anion) of an acid [2]. Sodium chloride (NaCl), for example, is the most abundant salt in the ocean. Normally, the salinity should be around 33-37 g/L. The salinity is important to be considered since it affects the osmotic pressure. A higher salt concentration /L can put stress on fish gills, and a lower concentration will allow disease-causing bacteria to grow.

Besides salinity, another important parameter for water quality is pH. pH is the negative logarithm of H+ concentration, which can represent the acidity of water. The acid in the ocean mostly comes from hydrogen ions dissociated from dissolved carbon dioxide (CO2). In an aquarium, the acid can also come from the nitrification process, which is a metabolism carried out by nitrobacteria that produce HNO3 [3]. The accumulation of acid through these two main processes will cause pH to decrease. When the pH is too low, fish will get stressed due to respiration difficulties, and the acid will also harm their gill and intervene in metabolism. However, if the pH is too high, fish will also be stressed due to ammonium toxin. This is because, under high pH, the NH3/NH4+ in water will be stabled in the NH3 form, which will seriously damage the fish neuro system. The pH in the aquarium should be within the natural range, which is empirically from 7.6 to 8.4.

Alkalinity ( KH) is another parameter used for marine aquariums. Alkalinity refers to the buffering capacity whereas KH refers to the carbonate hardness of seawater by measuring the concentration of carbonate species (HCO3- and CO32-) [4]. HCO3- and CO32- are formed in the ocean when CO2 dissolves and reacts with H2O to form H2CO3, which then dissociates twice into CO32-. Since the carbonate species are the major contributor to the buffering capacity of seawater, KH can be used to represent the alkalinity.

The KH (carbonates concentration) can also be called “carbonate hardness” since it represents the concentration of metal ions (e.g. Ca2+) that are ready to react with the HCO3- and CO32- [4]. When they react, the Ca2+ can be withdrawn from the water by forming solid CaCO3, which is why KH is also called “temporary hardness”. There are two major chemical equations that govern this reversible conversion:

1. CaCO3 ⇌ Ca2+ + CO32-

2. CaCO3(s) + H2CO3 ⇌ Ca2+ + 2HCO3-

These reactions participate in many biological processes, which include the calcification of and metabolism of marine organisms. In short, KH is important due to two reasons: The first reason is that carbonate species can combine with extra H+ and stabilize the pH, and seawater with higher KH can cope with more abrupt pH changes. The second reason is that carbonates help to maintain important biological processes, which also require a suitable pH. However, the concentration of base should not be too high since it can break the equilibrium and shift pH higher than optimum. Empirically, the KH in the aquarium should be maintained around 8 to 12 dKH, with 1 dKH 17.9 ppm [5].

NO3- and PO43- are called nutrient salt, and they are the fundamental nutrients for all living organisms. These ions are generated by nitrification and mineralization, which the two terms represent the conversion of organic molecules (amino acid and ATP) into inorganic salts (NO3- and PO43-). If the nutrient salts accumulate, it will lead to eutrophication, which can promote bacterial and algal growth. The high nutrient salt concentration is also toxic to fish and corals, but the exact threshold is unclear. To control the concentration of the nutrient salts, bioremediation is a method of choice. Various bacteria that hitch a ride on rocks to CWLC can control the nutrient salt concentration by biodegradation. Also, the algae in the tank will also uptake nutrient salts and their precursors for photosynthesis. In the aquarium, the concentrations of these salts are controlled as low as possible. Empirically, PO43- is controlled to be less than 0.05 ppm, and NO3- is controlled to be less than 20 ppm. Some veterans may scoff at this and declare that fish are just fine in their eutrophicated tanks. However, despite the complicated theories arguing why we should control the nutrient salts, the best explanation is simple: we should control it because the concentrations of these salts are low in nature.

Water quality and Fish’s Physical health in CWLC aquarium

With support from HKU Learning Environment Services (LES), our group tested the water sample in the CWLC for three consecutive weeks and analyzed the discussed parameters of water quality. The experiment was carried out from October 7th to 28th, 2021, and the water sample was collected every night between 18:00 and 22:00.

According to the result, the average pH is 7.75 (STDEV = 0.1135), which lies within the empirical range (7.6 to 8.4). However, 7.75 is close to 7.6, suggesting the water is more acidic than average. As we have discussed, carbonate species are responsible for buffering the pH. Therefore, it is possible that KH in the tank is lower than normal.

According to the result, the average KH is 4.30 dKH (STDEV = 0.2714), which is much lower than the empirical range (8 to 12 dKH). CO32- and HCO3- deficiency may also cause the hardness of water to decrease, and organisms cannot metabolize calcium efficiently. Though fish may not be affected, stony corals may undergo bleaching and tissue necrosis.

The concentrations of nutrient salts NO3- and PO43- are also tested. For PO43-, the concentration is 0.29 ppm (STDEV = 0.0466). This shows that the PO43- in the CWLC aquarium is well-controlled, but there is still a distance from the optimum. As for NO3-, the concentration is 25.63 ppm (STDEV = 3.0127). The concentration of NO3- largely fluctuated twice during the first 13 days but remained quite steady at around 25 ppm on other days. The reason is not yet understood, but empirically we can neglect the extremes and take it as 25.00 ppm.

Suggestions and Summary

Based on our observation, the deviation of these parameters has not caused severe problems for fish in CWLC. But as I said before, we should control them according to what nature teaches us.

As for the deficiency in KH, more supplements can be added each time based on the descriptive literature. The amount and period of dosage need to be supported by calculation; otherwise, it may have adverse consequences.

As for the control of nutrient salts, using an external tank containing higher-class algae or directly buying an ATS module can achieve further reduction. Significantly, the lighting system in these external assemblies should be kept on 24 hours a day, considering the pH fluctuation due to plant respiration at night.

In sum, the life of fish in CWLC is good from both mental and physical perspectives. CWLC can consider increasing the KH supplement to buffer the pH and remove the extra nutrient salts with higher-class algae or ATS. As the water in the tank gets closer to nature, fish will show more graceful postures.
