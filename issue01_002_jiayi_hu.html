When Virus Meet Cancer: A New Possibility for Cancer Therapy?

Hu Jiayi
School of Biomedical Sciences, Faculty of Science, University of Hong Kong, HK SAR
Figure 1. Oncolytic viruses killing cancer cells.[1]

When you think of viruses, names that come into your minds can be coronavirus, HIV, or even a computer virus if you are not interested in biology. But whatever it is, you don’t have good impressions of it. Biological viruses can hijack a host cell and replicate themselves according to their genetic information. Then, the new viruses will release by dissolving the host cell, causing cell death while infecting new cells. However, have you ever thought of using these effects to cure diseases? Not just usual bacterial infections, but the dreaded cancer!

With the development of medicine, scientists decided to take advantage of the infectious, invasive, and reproductive ability of viruses. Just like the saying “fight fire with fire”, we can also “attack disease with disease”. Now, the opponent that people have found for the virus is cancer.

What is cancer?

Cancer is a disease caused by uncontrolled division of abnormal cells in almost any organ or tissue of the body. “All cells come from cells.” Under the control of genes, proteins, etc., most cells in human body produce daughter-cells orderly by dividing during their life cycle. However, if the division process gets out of control, normal cells can become cancer cells that grow out of bounds, infiltrating and destroying normal body tissues. To this day, cancer remains an elusive target for human medicine.

What is oncolytic virus?

The class of viruses that can preferentially infect and kill tumor cells is called oncolytic viruses (OV)[[2] Daoyuan Xie Yaomei Tian, Li Yang, "Engineering strategies to enhance oncolytic viruses in cancer immunotherapy," Signal Transduction and Targeted Therapy 7 (2022).][2]. The word “preferentially” is the key to the action of OV and is a major research challenge. OV should be designed to infect cancer cells without harming normal cells, which is called “targeting”. It’s like a precision guide missile that targets the enemy (cancer cells), marks and hits the enemy without harming the innocent (normal cells).

How to achieve the precise targeting of oncolytic viruses?

There is too little hope of finding a natural virus that targets cancer cells. Scientists must modify the virus so that it can distinguish normal cells and cancer cells, which means targeting the unique features of cancer cells. One difference is that cancer cells tend to have overexpressed receptors that bind to ligands, and such bindings are highly specific. Figuratively, a cancer cell holds a lock with a specific keyhole (receptor) and only the specific key (ligand) can open this lock. Now, we can add this special key to the “surface” of virus through gene modification, so the virus with the key can only open the lock of cancer cells, but not normal cells.

How do oncolytic viruses kill the tumor cells?

Oncolytic viruses are specific, targeting those specific receptors that are overexpressed in tumor cells, then invading the virus into the tumor cells. Although OVs themselves can cause the death of cancer cells, they play a more important role in awakening our body’s own immune response, which can be a broader and more powerful attack against cancer cells. Non-specific immunity is an innate means for humans to fight against invading pathogenic microorganisms. However, they have trouble recognizing cancer cells. At this point, OVs appear like scouts to show the way for non-specific immunity. After infecting the tumor cells, OVs replicate and perform the ability of virus to cause cell lysis (dissolution or destruction of cells)[[3] "Oncolytic Virus Therapy: A Promising Approach to Immunotherapy," ESCOVACCIXCELL, 2019, accessed July 11, 2023.][3]. Release of tumor antigens and cytokines by cell lysis triggers nonspecific immune response and recruit more immune cells (e.g., T-cells) to continue killing the remaining tumor cells[[4] Danni Lin, "Oncolytic virotherapy: basic principles, recent advances and future directions."][4]. Large numbers of immune cells outside the tumor are induced and infiltrate inside the tumor[[5] João Manuel dos Santos Otto Hemminki, Akseli Hemminki, "Oncolytic viruses for cancer immunotherapy," Journal of Hematology & Oncology 13 (2020).][5], and the activated nonspecific immune cells kill and phagocytose those infected but unkilled tumor cells.

OVs can also invade the tumor’s vascular endothelial cells, causing cell apoptosis (a type of cell death), in turn, affecting the blood supply to the tumor[[6] "Oncolytic Virus Therapy: A Promising Approach to Immunotherapy," ESCOVACCIXCELL, 2019, accessed July 11, 2023.][6]. This is equivalent to cutting off the enemy’s food supply in a war, which is a powerful move to defeat the enemy. To summarize, oncolytic viruses can selectively infect tumor cells, replicate in large numbers and eventually destroy them.

How far have we gone?

The most studied oncolytic viruses include Adenovirus and Herpes Simplex Virus Type 1 (HSV-1), and there are many advantages of selecting them as the main studying targets of OVs. Firstly, the genomes of these two viruses are relatively large, so there’s enough space for scientists to make modifications in their genomes. Secondly, they have glycoproteins that can target glycolphilic cancer cells. Lastly, they have specific mechanisms for specific cancer microenvironments and cancer cell types, so they can further remodel the cancer microenvironments and promote long-term tumor-specific immunity[[7] Louisa S Chard Dunmall Lihong Wang, Zhenguo Cheng, Yaohe Wang, "Remodeling the tumor microenvironment by oncolytic viruses: beyond oncolysis of tumor cells for cancer treatment," J Immunother Cancer 10 (2022).][7]. Currently, oncolytic viruses have achieved excellent results in mice experiments, showing its effectiveness in cancer therapy[[8] David M. Miller Sophia Z. Shalhout, Kevin S. Emerick, Howard L. Kaufman, "Therapy with oncolytic viruses: progress and challenges," nature reviews 20 (2023).][8]. We can wait for its good news in clinical research.

What is the significance of oncolytic viruses?

Despite significant medical advances in recent years, the main options for treating cancer remain surgery, chemotherapy and radiation therapy. Limitations are still unresolved for aggressive cancers, also known as malignant tumors, where the success rate of cure and late resistance are still unanswered questions. OVs can target cancer cells and further trigger immune responses, which can be a powerful adjunct to the still-powerful but less efficient conventional therapies. Thus, OVs can not only fight alone, but can also be the strongest helper. Oncolytic viruses offer new possibilities and ideas in enriching the arsenal of cancer treatment.

Now, you do not have to be afraid and disgusted by the mention of viruses, because clever humans are saving lives with these dangerous gadgets!
